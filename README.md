# MIT 8.03SC — Vibrations and Waves

This repository contains my complete conceptual and problem-based work for **MIT 8.03SC: Vibrations and Waves**, developed as preparation for advanced study in quantum mechanics and theoretical physics.

The emphasis throughout is on understanding **structure, symmetry, and physical interpretation** of wave phenomena rather than treating oscillations as isolated mechanical problems. The course is approached as a bridge between classical mechanics and modern physics.

---

## Scope of the Course

MIT 8.03SC develops the physics of oscillatory systems and wave motion starting from simple harmonic motion and progressing toward continuous systems and wave propagation. The material covered includes:

- Simple harmonic motion and driven oscillations  
- Damping, resonance, and energy flow in oscillators  
- Coupled oscillators and normal modes  
- Eigenvalue methods in physical systems  
- Discrete lattices and collective motion  
- Continuous limits and the wave equation  
- Traveling and standing waves  
- Boundary conditions and mode quantization  
- Fourier decomposition and spectral representation  
- Wave packets and group velocity  
- Dispersion and physical interpretation of ω(k) relations  
- Reflection and transmission at interfaces  
- Energy transport in waves and conservation principles  

A central theme of the course is that **waves arise naturally from coupled degrees of freedom**, and that normal modes provide the natural language for describing physical systems.

---

## Conceptual Emphasis

The course was studied with the perspective that vibrations and waves form the mathematical and conceptual precursor to quantum mechanics. Particular focus was placed on:

- Normal modes as eigenstates of physical systems  
- Boundary conditions as the origin of quantization  
- Fourier analysis as decomposition into physical modes  
- Dispersion relations as dynamical fingerprints of media  
- The transition from discrete systems to continuum fields  
- Energy transport and locality in wave motion  

Oscillations are treated not merely as mechanical phenomena but as the first appearance of field-like behavior in physics.

## Problem-Solving Approach

Across the course, a consistent methodology was followed:

1. Identify the physical degrees of freedom  
2. Write equations of motion from force or energy principles  
3. Search for symmetry and translational invariance  
4. Reduce dynamics to normal modes whenever possible  
5. Interpret solutions physically before algebraic manipulation  
6. Examine limiting cases (long wavelength, continuum limit, weak coupling)  
7. Connect mathematical structure to observable behavior  

The goal is clarity of mechanism rather than algebraic complexity.
